\chapter{Methods}
\label{ch:methods}

Our work examines how reinforcement learning agents develop their
internal features, what determines how well those features capture
the task, and what can be done to improve them. \Cref{sec:agents}
introduces two agents that develop features differently, and
\cref{sec:experimental-design} defines the experiment that tests
what accounts for the difference. \Cref{sec:analysis-methods}
describes how we analyze what the features capture.

\section{Agents}
\label{sec:agents}

Data-Efficient Rainbow (DER) \citep{vanHasselt2019} set the first
baseline for learning from limited data by tuning Rainbow's
hyperparameters.\footnote{SimPLe \citep{kaiser2020model} introduced
the Atari-100K benchmark with a model-based approach.
\citet{vanHasselt2019} showed that tuning Rainbow's hyperparameters
alone matched its performance without architectural changes.}
Every component of Rainbow serves a single objective, improving how
the agent performs.

\smallskip
Self-Predictive Representations (SPR) \citep{schwarzer2021dataefficient}
adds a second objective to the same architecture. The encoder learns
to predict future representations through two additional components:
a predictor and a transition model
(\cref{tab:agents}).\footnote{We use the JAX reimplementation from
\citet{nikishin2022primacy}, which uses 512 dimensions and a separate
projection layer. The original PyTorch version uses 256 dimensions.}
Predicting the future shapes the agent's features to capture how
observations change over time.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\vspace{0.5em}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Component        & DER                          & SPR \\
\midrule
Predictor        & None & Fully connected, 512 units \\
Transition model & None & Two conv layers, 64 channels \\
Target update    & Hard copy every 8{,}000 steps & Moving average every step \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\vspace{0.5em}
\caption{Components that differ between DER and SPR. Both agents
  share the Nature CNN encoder and a 512-unit projection layer.}
\label{tab:agents}
\end{table}

The predictor, transition model, and prediction loss form the
\emph{latent prediction system}. We apply the same system to both
agents to test whether predicting the future improves features in
different training contexts.
